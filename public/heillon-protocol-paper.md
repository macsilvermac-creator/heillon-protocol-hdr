# HEILLON
## Decision Verification Protocol

---

## 1. Introduction

Autonomous systems—AI agents, automated workflows, and software-driven operations—increasingly execute actions that have institutional, financial, and regulatory consequences. Financial transfers, infrastructure changes, system governance, and operational decisions are today performed by machines without a standardized mechanism to verify whether those decisions were legitimate *before* execution.

This creates a structural risk: institutions lack a verification layer between intent and execution. The HEILLON protocol addresses this gap by introducing a decision verification infrastructure that validates proposed actions against institutional policies prior to any execution.

---

## 2. The Verification Gap

Current systems typically rely on *post-execution* mechanisms to ensure compliance and accountability:

- **Audits** — Retrospective review of actions after they have occurred.
- **Policies** — Rules enforced at the application layer, often bypassed or interpreted inconsistently by automated agents.
- **Logs** — Records that document what happened, but do not prove that the decision was verified in advance.

These mechanisms are reactive. They detect violations after the fact. They do not prevent unauthorized or non-compliant execution from occurring. The verification gap is the absence of a mandatory, cryptographically verifiable checkpoint that must be passed before an autonomous system is permitted to execute a decision.

---

## 3. The HEILLON Model

HEILLON introduces the model: **Intent → Validation → Execution**.

1. **Intent** — A proposed action generated by software, an AI agent, or an automated system. The intent describes *what* the system intends to do.

2. **Validation** — The intent is submitted to the HEILLON verification pipeline. No execution occurs until the pipeline completes successfully. The pipeline evaluates the intent against authority, policy, risk, and governance rules.

3. **Execution** — Only after validation succeeds does the system receive authorization to execute. Each verified decision produces a cryptographically signed proof artifact (HDR) that can be audited and verified independently.

Verification must happen *before* execution. This is the core architectural principle of the protocol.

---

## 4. Decision Verification Pipeline

The verification pipeline consists of four gates. Each gate must be passed for the decision to be authorized.

### Authority Gate

Validates that the actor proposing the intent has the institutional authority to perform the proposed action. Checks credentials, roles, and permissions against the institutional directory.

### Policy Gate

Evaluates the intent against institutional policies. Ensures that the proposed action complies with business rules, regulatory requirements, and organizational constraints.

### Risk Gate

Assesses the risk profile of the proposed action. Flags high-risk or anomalous intents for additional review. May escalate to human approval when thresholds are exceeded.

### Governance Gate

Confirms that the decision aligns with governance frameworks, audit trails, and organizational mandates. Ensures that the decision can be traced and justified within the institutional structure.

The gates operate in sequence. A decision that fails any gate is not authorized. Only decisions that pass all gates proceed to execution and receive an HDR artifact.

---

## 5. HDR Artifact

The **HDR (Heillon Decision Record)** is a cryptographic proof artifact produced for every verified decision. It contains:

- **Intent Hash** — Cryptographic digest of the proposed intent.
- **Decision Outcome** — AUTHORIZED, DENIED, or PENDING REVIEW.
- **Policy Version** — The version of policies applied during verification.
- **Ledger Event** — Reference to the immutable record in the Decision Ledger.
- **Timestamp** — When the verification occurred.
- **Verification Signature** — Cryptographic signature attesting that the decision was validated by the protocol.

The HDR provides a verifiable proof that a decision was validated *before* execution. It can be stored, audited, and shared without relying on application logs or internal systems. The artifact structure is standardized to support interoperability across different implementations.

---

## 6. Institutional Impact

HEILLON shifts accountability from reactive to structural:

- **Pre-execution verification** — Decisions are validated before machines act. Non-compliant intents are blocked rather than corrected after the fact.

- **Verifiable proof** — The HDR artifact provides cryptographic evidence of verification. Institutions can demonstrate to regulators, auditors, and stakeholders that decisions were validated according to protocol.

- **Audit trail** — The Decision Ledger maintains an immutable, chronological record of all verified decisions. This supports forensic analysis and compliance reporting.

- **Structural legitimacy** — The protocol introduces legitimacy as a property of the infrastructure, not as an add-on or policy layer. Verification becomes mandatory, not optional.

---

## 7. Example Use Case: Automated Treasury Transfer

An automated treasury system proposes a transfer of funds from one account to another. Without HEILLON:

- The system executes the transfer and logs the action.
- Audits and policies are applied after execution.
- Violations are detected only when logs are reviewed.

With HEILLON:

1. The system generates an **Intent** describing the transfer (amount, currency, source, destination, actor).

2. The intent is submitted to the **Verification Pipeline**. It passes through Authority Gate (does the actor have permission?), Policy Gate (does the amount comply with limits?), Risk Gate (is the transaction flagged as high-risk?), and Governance Gate (is the action aligned with treasury governance?).

3. If all gates pass, the decision is **authorized**. An **HDR artifact** is generated and recorded in the Decision Ledger.

4. The treasury system receives the HDR as proof of authorization and **executes** the transfer. The HDR is stored with the transaction for audit purposes.

If any gate fails, the transfer is not executed. The institution retains control over automated financial operations through verifiable, pre-execution validation.

---

## 8. Conclusion

HEILLON introduces structural legitimacy for autonomous systems. By requiring verification before execution and producing cryptographically verifiable proof for each decision, the protocol closes the gap between institutional authority and automated action. It is infrastructure, not product: a verification layer that institutions can adopt to ensure that software, AI agents, and automated workflows execute only decisions that have been validated according to institutional rules.

The model **Intent → Validation → Execution** defines the operational sequence. The HDR artifact provides the proof. Together, they create a foundation for accountable autonomous systems.
